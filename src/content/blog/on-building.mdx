There's a paradox at the heart of software engineering: we build things meant to last in a medium that changes constantly.

## The Impermanence Problem

Every framework you learn today will be obsolete in five years. Every architecture decision you make is a bet against an uncertain future. And yet — we build. We build with care, with craft, with attention to detail that often goes unseen.

Why?

## Craft as Practice

I think the answer lies in separating the *act* of building from the *artifact* that results. The code may be rewritten, the architecture may be replaced, but the thinking — the careful reasoning about trade-offs, the empathy for future developers, the elegance of a well-designed interface — that persists.

It persists in you. In how you approach the next problem. In the patterns you recognize, the mistakes you don't repeat, the intuitions you develop.

## The Stoic Developer

Marcus Aurelius wrote: "The object of life is not to be on the side of the majority, but to escape finding oneself in the ranks of the insane."

In software, the majority often chases the new — the latest framework, the trending architecture, the hype cycle. The craftsperson asks different questions:

- Does this solve the actual problem?
- Will this be maintainable by someone who isn't me?
- Is this the simplest thing that could work?

## Building Broken

At Broken, we're building a no-code platform. The irony isn't lost on me — a developer building tools so that others don't need to code. But I see it differently. We're not replacing developers. We're expanding who gets to build.

The best tools disappear. They become so natural that you forget they're there. That's what we're working toward.
